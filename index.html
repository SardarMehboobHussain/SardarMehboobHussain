About Me

I am Sardar Mehboob Hussain, a researcher in Biomedical Engineering focusing on Explainable Artificial Intelligence (XAI) and Deep Learning for Medical Imaging. My work bridges AI transparency and healthcare innovation, developing intelligent diagnostic systems and image-guided robotic surgery solutions that enhance clinical decision-making.

I earned my PhD in Biomedical Engineering from the Polytechnic University of Bari, Italy. I currently work as a Deep Learning Engineer at Comau S.p.A., contributing to AI-based image analysis, model interpretability, and robotic automation. I have authored over 15 research papers and delivered numerous invited talks on XAI and robotic surgery.

From Code to Clinic: What I Learned Building AI for Real Doctors

I used to think a 99% accuracy on a test set was the finish line. Then I showed my model to a radiologist.

She looked at the output for 10 seconds and said: “Why did it flag this shadow? Is it a mass or just an artifact?”

That moment changed everything.

1. Accuracy ≠ Trust

In research, we optimize for numbers. In hospitals, doctors optimize for confidence. A model can be right 99 times — but if it fails in a way no human would, it’s useless.

2. Explanations Must Speak Medicine, Not Math

Heatmaps are pretty. But a surgeon doesn’t care about pixel gradients. They want to know:

  • Is this lesion spiculated?
  • Are there microcalcifications?
  • Why is this different from last month’s scan?
3. The Best Models Are Teachers

The future isn’t AI replacing doctors. It’s AI training them. I’ve seen junior residents spot anomalies faster after 10 minutes with an explainable system than after hours of textbooks.

I’m still learning. Every failed deployment, every confused clinician, every “but why?” is data.

Because in the end, the model doesn’t need to be perfect — it needs to be understood.

My Work: AI That Doctors Trust

I build AI for medical imaging and robotic surgery. The goal isn’t just accuracy — it’s trust.

As Goodhart’s Law warns: "When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure."

Doctors need to know why a model flags a tumor or guides a robot’s hand. I use explainable AI (XAI) to turn black-box predictions into clear, clinical insights.

From breast lesion analysis to real-time surgical support — my work bridges code and care. Because in medicine, understanding saves lives.
